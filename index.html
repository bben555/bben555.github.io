Policy Statement

Holistic Learning Policy for the Average Indigenous and Non-Indigenous mixed Classroom

This policy statement is designed for a mixed classroom environment with both Indigenous and non-Indigenous children, creating a space where diverse cultural perspectives are honored, with particular emphasis on Indigenous worldviews and ways of knowing.

Our Foundational Principles:

We believe that meaningful reconciliation should begin in early childhood that lead to a lifetime commitment. Our learning environment embraces the wisdom of Indigenous perspectives while creating space for all children to develop understanding, respect, and appreciation for Indigenous histories, cultures, and ways of knowing. We commit to:

  1. Land Acknowledgement whenever appropriate; Hence honoring the land on which our learning takes place through regular acknowledgment and teachings about the traditional territories
  2. Integrating Indigenous languages, stories, and cultural practices throughout our curriculum, not as isolated activities but as foundational elements of our daily learning.
  3. Provide funding and training to teachers and staff to learn about indigenous knowledge and teaching methods so they can bring it to the classroom.
  4. Adopting a complete educational approach mirroring that of indigenous curriculum that nurture the intellectual, physical, and emotional aspects of development.
  5. Creating continuous opportunities for families to participate in and contribute to our learning community.

Alignment with TRC Calls to Action:

This policy directly addresses several TRC Calls to Action related to education, including:

  • Call #64: Calling upon all levels of government that provide public funds to denominational schools to require such schools to provide an education on comparative religious studies, which must include a segment on 8 | Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada Aboriginal spiritual beliefs and practic
  • Call #62.3: Provide the necessary funding to Aboriginal schools to utilize Indigenous knowledge and teaching methods in classrooms.
  • Call #62.2: Provide the necessary funding to post-secondary institutions to educate teachers on how to integrate Indigenous knowledge and teaching methods into classrooms.
  • Call #62.1: Making age-appropriate curriculum on residential schools, Treaties, and Aboriginal peoples’ historical and contemporary contributions to Canada amandatory education requirement for Kindergarten to Grade Twelve students

Our approach recognizes that decolonization in early childhood education requires more than adding Indigenous content—it necessitates a fundamental shift in how we conceptualize teaching and learning, moving from Eurocentric frameworks toward more holistic, land-based, and relationship-centered approaches.

Classroom Design

Physical Environment

Assuming we can design any classroom from scratch, my ideal classroom would look a lot different from the average classrooms in place currently every school in Canada. The most striking difference would be the inclusion of outdoor space in every classroom through the form of a porch/greenhouse/balcony. By having indoor and outdoor spaces, it will be easier to incorporate demonstrations of indigenous knowledge such as singing, dancing, crafting, familization with elements of nature etc.

Indoor Elements:

  • A circular arrangement reflecting the medicine wheel
  • Natural materials predominate (wood, stone, plant fibers)
  • Flexible, movable furniture allowing for gathering in circles
  • Low shelving with natural materials and loose parts for exploration
  • A dedicated "Elder's corner" with comfortable seating for storytelling and knowledge sharing

Outdoor Elements:

  • A natural playscape with native plants, rocks, logs, and water features
  • A medicine wheel garden growing traditional plants used for teaching
  • A covered outdoor gathering space for circle time regardless of weather

Sensory Elements

Visual

  • Images of local Indigenous peoples, land acknowledgements
  • Artwork created by Indigenous artists reflecting traditional and contemporary expressions
  • Natural light prioritized over artificial lighting
  • Nature-based color palette reflecting seasons

Auditory

  • Natural acoustic properties with minimal artificial sounds
  • Wind chimes and indigenous drums
  • Moments of intentional silence for establishing mindfulness

Tactile

  • Variety of natural materials with different textures
  • Traditional weaving materials
  • Clay and natural modeling materials

Olfactory

  • Sacred medicines present but used only in appropriate ceremonial contexts
  • Natural plant aromas from indoor plants and healing herbs
  • Seasonal harvests and traditional foods prepared with children

Taste

  • Rotating traditional food offerings in the cafeteria.
  • Berries and edible plants in the outdoor space for identification

The Learning Activities

Our Relations: The Tree Family Circle

A recurring seasonal activity connecting children with trees as relations and teachers, following natural cycles while integrating traditional ecological knowledge.

Children develop relationships with specific trees throughout the seasons, learning through direct observation and connection with the natural world.

Seasons of Story: The Talking Circle Blanket

An activity centering Indigenous storytelling traditions while connecting children to seasonal cycles through collaborative creation of a storytelling blanket.

Children gather seasonal natural materials to create a collective storytelling blanket that represents their learning journey throughout the year.

Activity Process

  1. Beginning the Circle (Fall): Introduction to seasonal storytelling concepts
  2. Seasonal Story Collection: Gathering outdoors, listening to traditional stories
  3. Story Medicine: Learning how stories serve as medicine and teaching
  4. Culminating Ceremony: Year-end gathering to celebrate the completed blanket

Connection to Indigenous Worldviews

  • Honors oral traditions and intergenerational knowledge transmission
  • Recognizes stories as living entities carrying cultural wisdom
  • Follows seasonal protocols for when certain stories should be shared
  • Connects narrative traditions to natural cycles

Actions for Decolonization and Indigenization

Decolonization Actions

  1. Rethink timetables: Consider using a flexible daily schedule that follows natural cycles conforming to the season and children sleep-wake cycles rather than rigid clock-based schedules.
  2. Community Involvement for Curriculum Development: Invite indigenous parents, elders, and knowledge keepers to decolonize curriculum.
  3. Reconceptualize Assessment: Move away from percetanges, letter grades and other result based on Western milestones toward a holistic evaluation method that account for individuals effort.

Indigenization Actions

  1. Land-Based Learning Protocol: Establish a formal relationship with local Indigenous communities. Have the chiefs come give student talks and stories.
  2. Language Immersion Moments: Expose children to indigenous language.

Implementation Plan: The Language Revitalization Circle

Plan Details

This plan combines the decolonization and Indigenization actions through establishing an ongoing Language Revitalization Circle within the early learning environment.

Location and Setting:

  • A dedicated indoor language nest space designed with circular seating and natural materials
  • Outdoor gathering spaces on the land for contextual language learning
  • Virtual connections to other language revitalization initiatives when appropriate
  • Community spaces where languages are used authentically

Timeframe:

  • Daily language circles (15-30 minutes)
  • Weekly immersion sessions (2-3 hours)
  • Monthly community language gatherings (half-day)
  • Seasonal language celebrations aligned with traditional seasonal activities

Participation Structure:

  • Children participate through songs, games, stories, and daily activities conducted in Indigenous languages
  • Educators commit to learning alongside children as co-learners
  • Elders and Language Keepers lead sessions and provide guidance on pronunciation and cultural context
  • Families are invited to learn basic phrases and participate in take-home language activities
  • Community members with language knowledge are invited to share their expertise

Sensory Elements in Implementation

Visual Elements

  • Visual syllabic charts and vocabulary posters
  • Photographs of Indigenous language speakers from the community
  • Hand-drawn illustrated storybooks featuring local landmarks
  • Visual documentation of children's growing language use

Auditory Elements

  • Daily songs, chants, and prayers in Indigenous languages
  • Audio recordings of Elders speaking the language
  • Natural sounds from the land that correspond with specific vocabulary
  • Rhythmic games that incorporate language through movement

Tactile Elements

  • Handling of natural objects while learning their names
  • Creating traditional crafts while learning process words
  • Texture cards with corresponding vocabulary
  • Medicine pouch creation with items named in Indigenous language

Olfactory Elements

  • Seasonal plant harvesting with learning of plant names
  • Traditional food preparation with vocabulary learning
  • Sacred medicines introduced with proper protocols
  • Scent memory activities connecting language to aromas

Taste Elements

  • Traditional food tasting with vocabulary learning
  • Seasonal berry harvesting and preparation
  • Medicine tea preparation and tasting
  • Garden growing with vocabulary for plant stages

Evaluation Approach

Desired Outcomes

  1. Increased Everyday Language Use: Children and educators regularly incorporating Indigenous language words and phrases into daily communication.
  2. Community Capacity Building: Strengthened connections between the early learning center and language knowledge holders in the community.
  3. Pedagogical Transformation: Shift from Western-centric teaching methods toward Indigenous pedagogies guided by Elders and Knowledge Keepers.
  4. Family Engagement: Families learning alongside children and bringing language practice into home environments.
  5. Identity Affirmation: Indigenous children experiencing pride and connection to their cultural identity through language exposure.
  6. Cultural Understanding: Non-Indigenous children developing respect and appreciation for Indigenous languages and knowledge systems.
  7. Systemic Change: Development of a replicable model for language revitalization in early learning settings that challenges colonial educational frameworks.

Evaluation Methods

The evaluation approach incorporates Indigenous methodologies that value relationship, story, and collective reflection:

Regular Sharing Circles:

  • Monthly circles with children to gather their experiences through stories and artwork
  • Questions posed: "What new words do you like to use? How does it feel to speak these words? What stories have you learned through these words?"
  • Documentation through audio recording (with permission) and visual representations

Elder and Knowledge Keeper Guidance:

  • Quarterly gatherings with Elders to reflect on the program's direction
  • Questions posed: "Are we following proper protocols? What strengths do you see emerging? How can we better honor the language?"
  • Documentation through written reflections and implementation of recommendations

Family Story Gathering:

  • Semi-annual collection of family stories about language use at home
  • Questions posed through conversation: "What words or phrases has your child brought home? How has language learning affected your family relationships? What more would support your family's language journey?"
  • Documentation through family journals and recorded conversations (with permission)

Community Language Assessment:

  • Annual community gathering to assess language vitality
  • Collaborative mapping of where and how language is being used
  • Questions posed: "Where is the language living now that it wasn't before? What new contexts for language use have emerged? What barriers remain to language revitalization?"
  • Documentation through community-created maps and action plans
